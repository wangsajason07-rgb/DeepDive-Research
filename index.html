Main hypothesis

The core argument of this research

Dutch regulatory barriers are the primary obstacles preventing Physical Internet hub implementation in Dutch ports, making deployment within 12 months unfeasible without direct regulatory intervention.

Key findings from literature

What we already know

01

Technology is ready — the problem lies elsewhere

Level 4 AV technology for freight is proven in controlled scenarios. The Port of Rotterdam has been running autonomous terminal tractors since 2020. For hub-to-hub and node operations, technology is no longer the limiting factor.

Snelder et al. (2025) · Kempen & Meijeren (2025)
02

Vienna Convention creates a legal vacuum for Level 4

The 1968 Convention assumes a human driver at all times, creating a structural legal conflict for Level 4 autonomous operation under Dutch law. No national resolution has been enacted, unlike Germany and Sweden.

Vellinga (2019) · Tjin Tai (2024)
03

RDW assesses every AV application case-by-case

No standardised approval pathway exists for Level 4 in port or industrial zone environments. Each application is assessed individually, creating timeline uncertainty and discouraging both operator investment and insurance underwriting.

RDW (2025) · CAD Europe
04

Physical Internet demands continuous 24/7 autonomous operations

Port-scale PI hubs require uninterrupted automated container movement. Unlike road freight, human drivers cannot substitute — the AV dependency is a fundamental architectural requirement, making the regulatory blocker even more critical.

Ballot et al. (2020) · Nikitas et al. (2020)

Specific regulatory blockers
Blocker R1 — RDW approval process

Case-by-case approval with no standardised port pathway

No fast-track or standardised pathway exists for Level 4 in port/industrial zones. Every application is assessed individually by RDW, creating timeline uncertainty that discourages operator investment and makes insurance underwriting practically impossible.

Blocker R2 — Vienna Convention

1968 Vienna Convention mandates a human driver at all times

The Netherlands is legally bound by the Convention which requires human driver presence. Level 4 autonomous operation creates a liability vacuum that Dutch law has not resolved — unlike Germany (2021) and Sweden (2024) which enacted national legislation to circumvent this.

Blockers R3 & R4 — Liability & insurance chain

No liability framework → no insurance → no deployment

Unclear responsibility between manufacturer and operator makes insurers unwilling to offer coverage. Without insurance, operators cannot legally deploy AVs even when technology is fully ready. This chain — unclear liability → no insurance → no deployment — is the practical bottleneck that blocks Physical Internet hub implementation.

Data collection plan

Triangulated methodology

M1

Document analysis

15–20 publicly available policy documents plus 21 academic sources, coded R/T/E/C with sub-codes R1–R4. Frequency analysis identifies dominant barriers; timeline tracking tests 12-month feasibility.

M2

Comparative case analysis

Structured comparison of the Netherlands, Germany, and Sweden across five regulatory dimensions. Equal AV technology access means deployment gaps are attributed to regulatory differences.

M3

Regulatory readiness index

Each country scored per regulatory dimension, producing a measurable output quantifying how far the Netherlands lags. The index directly answers whether 12-month deployment is feasible and which gaps must close first.

M4

Interviews & survey (if access permits)

Semi-structured interviews with RDW officials, Port of Rotterdam managers, and AV operators. A short survey for broader barrier ranking. Supplementary to M1–M3; skipped if access is unavailable within the research window.

Research gap

What the literature leaves unanswered

Which barrier is worst?

Regulatory, technical, economic, and cultural barriers are identified — but no study ranks them for Dutch port/industrial zone operations specifically.

How exactly does Dutch regulation block?

Is it the slow RDW approval, unclear liability, or the insurance gap? The specific port-level mechanism remains unaddressed in existing literature.

Is 12-month deployment feasible?

No existing literature assesses whether PI hub implementation in Dutch ports is achievable within a defined short-term timeframe.

What specific changes are needed?

Problems are identified but the literature stops short of specifying concrete regulatory fixes that would actually enable deployment.

Vienna Convention opt-out?

Does the Netherlands need a national opt-out from the 1968 Convention to legally enable Level 4 AV operations in port zones?

RDW fast-track for port zones?

Could a dedicated fast-track process for controlled port environments unlock deployment without requiring full national legislation?

Desired outcomes

What this research will deliver

Barrier ranking by severity

A definitive ordering of regulatory, technical, economic, and cultural blockers specifically for Dutch port Physical Internet operations.

Recommendations for companies

Actionable guidance for port operators and logistics firms on what to monitor, prepare, and advocate for in anticipation of regulatory change.

Policy suggestions for the Netherlands

Specific changes — RDW fast-track, Road Traffic Act amendment, Vienna Convention national opt-out — that would enable 12-month deployment.

Regulatory readiness decision framework

A structured tool for assessing PI hub feasibility under current regulation vs. reformed scenarios, usable by both port operators and policymakers.
